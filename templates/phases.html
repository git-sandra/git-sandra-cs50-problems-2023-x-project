We can distinguish five phases which are: Early Phase 1 (formerly listed as Phase 0), Phase 1, Phase 2, Phase 3 and Phase 4. Not Applicable is used to describe trials without defined phases, including studies of devices or behavioral interventions. The phase is based on the study's objective, the number of participants and other characteristics.

  • Phase 1 focuses on the drug's safety. They are usually conducted with healthy volunteers and involve a small number of participants. The goal is to determine the drug's most frequent and serious adverse events (SAE).
  • Phase 2 of clinical studies gather preliminary data on whether a drug works in people who have a certain condition/disease. Participants receiving the drug may be compared to other subjects receiving a different treatment, usually an inactive substance, known also as a placebo or a different drug. In this phase safety as well as short-term adverse events (AE) are studied.
  • Phase 3 accumulate more information about a drug's safety and effectiveness by studying different populations and different dosages. These studies typically involve much more participants.
  • Phase 4 occurs after drug approval. It includes postmarket requirement and commitment studies that are required of or agreed to by the study sponsor. During this phase additional information about a drug's safety, efficacy or optimal use are gathered.
